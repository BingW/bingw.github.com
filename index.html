Types of Variants

  • Multiple adjacent or clustered polymorphisms
  • Insertions and deletions (Indels)

Separating true variants from artifacts

  • Ignore suspicious regions – areas where too many reads mapped given the expected coverage
  • Require three reads on each strand

Tradeoffs of Sp VS Sn

  • Sn = TP/(TP + FN)
  • Sn = Sensitivity: what portion of the total number of real variants did you detect?
  • Sp = TN/(TN + FP)
  • Sp = Specificity: what portion of the variants you discovered are really variants, not false positives?

Syntax

Features of origin Markdown中文

  • Head. use====or----under head.or use#or## or ### or #### … before head

  • Emphasize. use * or ** or _ or __ wrap text

Devin’s Pipeline

Sep 11th, 2012

  1. HMMER 1.8.4 -> detect high scoring matches to (an HMM created from an alignment of S.cerevisiae introns and flanking sequences.)

  2. Generated all ORFs above a context-dependent minimum size (60 bp at contigs ends and intron splice-sites, 300 bp at other locations), combined these with the putative splice-sites and selected the most upstream ATG as the putative start codon.

  3. Retained only possible gene models longer than 300 bp.

  4. Potential ORFs that spanned high-scoring matches to the splice site model were represented by multiple alternative gene models.

  5. At each locus, we aligned alternative models to the best available homolog (typically from S. cerevisiae) using Exonerate (Slater and Birney 2005) (-model affine:global -exhaustive 1 -bestn 1) and selected the model with the highest score.

  6. Sought positive evidence for all remaining gene models by BLAST (Altschul et al. 1997) against a local database of:
    • yeast proteins assembled from completely sequenced yeast genomes (Wood et al. 2002; Cliften et al. 2003 ; Dujon et al. 2004; Kellis et al. 2004; Souciet et al. 2009),
    • the NCBI nr protein database
    • S. cerevisiae Ty and LTR sequences downloaded from Saccharomyces Genome Database (Engel et al. 2010) (SGD, http:// www.yeastgenome.org)

  7. Use HMMER3 (Eddy 2009) to search our gene models against a database of 4704 protein HMMs derived from the Yeast Gene Order Browser (Byrne and Wolfe 2006) (YGOB, http://wolfe.gen.tcd.ie/ygob).

  8. In order to recover small genes or rapidly evolving genes, we subjected all presumed intergenic regions to low stringency BLAST searches against our local database of yeast proteins and searched genomic regions predicted by synteny to contain YGOB matches with our YGOB-HMM library.

  9. To detect potential novel genes, we used linear discriminant analysis to assign ORFs lacking homology to genes in the YGOB or yeast ortholog databases a “coding probability” based on whether their codon usage frequencies more closely resembled confirmed protein-coding genes or confirmed intergenic regions.

  10. Eliminated poorly supported gene models that overlapped well-supported models and merged neighboring models that were likely to be fragments of the same gene.

  11. The fragmented gene models were typically created by scaffold gaps or frameshifts (either real or due to sequencing errors).

  12. The remaining models were stratified according to the quality of the supporting evidence and comprised our initial protein-coding gene set.

  13. We used tRNA-ScanSE to identify tRNA genes (Lowe and Eddy 1997).

different identifier for phredwiki

A software for thisFastQC

Genome-scale Sequence Analysis

  • De nove assembly
  • Templated assembly
  • Read mapping or alignment to a reference genome

Assemblers

overlap graph assemblers:

  • Screen out known repeats and set them aside for later
  • Infer repetitiveness based on coverage
  • First assemble unambiguous overlaps, then resolve repeats using mate pairs

De Bruijn K-mers assemblers problems:

  • Require large amount of memory to store graph
  • Not as easy to parallelize as overlap assemblers

How many clones/reads do we need?

  • G = Genome Length
  • L = Sequence Read Length
  • N = Number of Sequences Collected
  • T = Number of Basepairs of Overlap Needed

Number of Contigs = $N \cdot e^{-\frac{LN}{G} \cdot (1-\frac{T}{L})}$ (LW prediction)

Gap Closure Strategies

  • Primer walk to sequence the rest of linking clones that span a scaffold gap
  • Primer walk off clones at the ends of contigs for which there is no linking information
  • PCR based on your best guess at contig order (comparison to other closely related genomes, predicted genes at the end of genomes, anything else you can come up with)
  • Combinatorial PCR with primers designed at the end of each contig
